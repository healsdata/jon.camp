*Part four of seven in a [series on agentic coding and WIP management](/tags/ai-vs-wip/).*

In 1865, the economist William Stanley Jevons noticed something uncomfortable[^1]: As steam engines became more fuel-efficient, England burned more coal, not less. Cheaper energy per unit of output unlocked applications that hadn't been economically viable before. Aggregate coal demand went up. In the same vein, more fuel-efficient cars led to more driving. Higher-yield crops led to more land cleared for farming.

Software is not exempt from the idea that, wherever efficiency improves, demand expands to consume it. And then some. 

> your org rarely has good ideas. ideas being expensive to implement was actually helping.
> 
> -- Dax Raad, developer behind OpenCode

When AI tools reduce the cost per feature, organizations respond by expecting more features from the same, or less[^2], developers. The expectation baseline rises. Total developer workload goes up. 

Jevons was here first.

This isn't an individual failure. It's a systemic effect. Even disciplined developers who maintain personal WIP limits get swept up in organizational expectations that recalibrate around AI's output levels. The Multitudes data makes this concrete[^3]; AI adoption correlated with 27.2% more PRs merged and a 19.6% rise in out-of-hours commits. The efficiency gain went to volume.

Addy Osmani's factory model piece[^4] offers the software-specific numbers. Since AI tools became mainstream, roughly 40% more websites exist, 50% more iOS apps, and 35% more GitHub pushes per period. Generation expanded dramatically. 

A Qatalog and Cornell study found something complementary[^5]. Switching between tools alone costs 9.5 minutes of concentration recovery time per switch. The more tasks AI enables, the more context-switching workers face, and the more cognitive capacity gets consumed by the overhead of doing more things faster.

Jevons tells us that efficiency gains require active demand governance to actually reduce burden. Without it, you don't get less work. You get a higher baseline of what counts as normal.

Tool adoption without demand management isn't a productivity improvement. It's a demand expansion mechanism.
